Terminology

Key Terms

Understanding the language of disability equity is the first step toward meaningful change.

The standard by which disabled people have access to cultural experiences of equivalent quality and dignity to those available to non-disabled people. Goes well beyond ramp access or a single interpreted performance.

The way disability intersects with race, class, gender, and other identities to create overlapping and compounded forms of disadvantage. Effective accessibility work must account for these intersections — not treat disability as a single, uniform experience.

The design of environments, programs, and content that can be used by all people without adaptation or specialized design. In cultural contexts, this means building access in from the start — not retrofitting it afterward.

A core disability rights principle: disabled people must be involved in decisions that affect them — not just consulted as an afterthought. For cultural organizations, this means including disabled people in leadership, programming, and governance, not just as audience members.

The view that disability is caused by barriers in society (physical, attitudinal, systemic), not by a person's impairment. Contrasts with the medical model, which locates the "problem" in the individual. The social model shapes how CulturalAccessNYC thinks about our work.
